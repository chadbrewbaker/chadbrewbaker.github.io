Horn clause solver

Positive Datalog — facts, definite Horn rules, and queries — evaluated to the least fixpoint by a semi-naive engine compiled to WebAssembly (8.6 KB, embedded in this page). Facts like edge(a,b)., rules like path(X,Y) :- edge(X,Z), path(Z,Y)., queries like ?- path(a,X). Variables start with a capital letter; 0-ary predicates give you propositional HORNSAT.

How it works, and where it comes from

1 Horn clauses and the least model

A definite Horn clause has exactly one positive literal: h ∨ ¬b₁ ∨ … ∨ ¬bₖ, more readably h ← b₁ ∧ … ∧ bₖ. Alfred Horn isolated the class in 1951 [1]; its computational charm, made precise by van Emden and Kowalski in 1976 [2], is that a set of definite clauses always has a unique least model, computable as the fixpoint of the “immediate consequence” operator: start from the facts and apply every rule until nothing new appears. That is exactly what this page does; a query then just inspects the fixpoint. For propositional Horn clauses, satisfiability is decidable in linear time by counting unmet body literals (Dowling–Gallier, 1984 [3]) — here the 0-ary case of the same forward chaining.

2 Semi-naive evaluation

The naive fixpoint recomputes every rule against every fact each round. The semi-naive refinement — standard in the deductive-database literature since the mid-1980s (Bancilhon [4]; see also Ullman [5] and the Ceri–Gottlob–Tanca survey [6]) — observes that a rule can only derive something new if at least one body atom matches a fact derived in the previous round. The engine keeps each relation as an append-only tuple store; the delta is just a range of indices, and each rule is evaluated once per body position with that atom restricted to the delta. Duplicates are suppressed by a hash table per relation, which is also what guarantees termination: Datalog without function symbols has a finite Herbrand base.

3 The WASM engine

The evaluator is ~300 lines of freestanding C compiled with clang to wasm32 — no libc, a bump allocator over linear memory, and integer-only data: the page interns every symbol to a u32 and serializes rules to a flat i32 array (negative numbers are variables), so the hot join loops never touch a string. Joins are nested loops with two accelerations: the delta atom is enumerated first, and every relation maintains a per-column hash index (value → chain of tuples), so an atom whose column is already bound costs a hash probe instead of a scan. On this machine that closes the transitive closure of a 1000-node chain — 499,500 derived tuples — in roughly a tenth of a second; the benchmark button reproduces it live. The design is a miniature of what production Datalog engines such as Soufflé [7] do at scale with compiled relational algebra and B-tree/trie indexes.

4 Scope

This is positive Datalog: no negation, no function symbols, no arithmetic. Those are principled omissions — stratified negation and built-ins each deserve their own careful semantics — and they keep the guarantee that every program terminates with a unique least model. Unsafe rules (a head variable missing from the body) are rejected at parse time with an explanation rather than evaluated wrongly.
